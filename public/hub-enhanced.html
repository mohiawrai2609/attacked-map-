FortiBleed credential leak: at least 50 named Indonesian institutions (PLN, Telkom, Ministry of Manpower) reported exposed in global Fortinet firewall compromise

PLN (Perusahaan Listrik Negara), Telkom Indonesia, Kementerian Ketenagakerjaan (Kemenaker), plus state and private banks and major e-commerce platforms · Indonesia · Government (National / Federal)

FortiBleed is a global credential-exposure campaign against internet-facing Fortinet FortiGate firewalls and FortiOS SSL VPN gateways. On 13 June 2026, security researcher Volodymyr "Bob" Diachenko publicly disclosed an exposed threat-actor server holding a validated database of administrator and SSL VPN credentials for roughly 73,932 FortiGate URLs (later figures cite ~75,000 and up to 86,644 devices) across 194 countries and 21,000+ domains — approximately half of all internet-reachable Fortinet firewalls. Kevin Beaumont and Hudson Rock independently validated that sampled admin credentials were authentic. Crucially, the campaign does NOT rely on a single new zero-day: attackers assembled credentials from earlier Fortinet breach dumps (CVE-2022-40684, CVE-2023-27997, CVE-2024-55591) and infostealer logs, validated them against live devices, then used compromised firewalls as passive listening posts to harvest fresh SSL VPN traffic. Because there is no patch that closes the exposure, defenders must rotate all admin/VPN credentials and enforce MFA. On 23-24 June 2026, Indonesian outlets Tempo and Sumbawanews reported, citing digital-forensics practitioner Alfons Tanujaya of PT Vaksincom, that at least 50 Indonesian FortiGate-using institutions appear in the dataset — including state electricity utility PLN, telecom incumbent Telkom Indonesia, the Ministry of Manpower (Kemenaker), and unnamed state and private banks and major e-commerce platforms. As of this record, the named Indonesian entities had not individually confirmed or denied exposure, and no BSSN/Kominfo statement had been reported; the Indonesian attribution rests on a single named expert relayed through two outlets, so institution-level exposure is REPORTED rather than independently CONFIRMED.

Cyberattack forces three major Iranian state banks to suspend card services nationwide

Bank Melli, Bank Saderat and Bank Tejarat (Iran) · Iran · Banking (Diversified & Universal)

On 23-24 June 2026, a cyberattack targeting card-processing systems forced Iran's three largest state-owned commercial banks - Bank Melli, Bank Saderat and Bank Tejarat - into a temporary nationwide suspension of card-based retail-payment services. ATMs, point-of-sale (POS) terminals and mobile banking apps tied to the card rails went offline, online payments became impossible, and citizens reported having to pay cash at fuel stations because the petrol-station payment network was affected. This was the second wave in two weeks: on 13-14 June 2026 the same banks plus the Export Development Bank of Iran (EDBI) were hit when attackers struck a shared core-banking communications/service layer operated by the Informatics Services Corporation (ISC) that also underpins parts of Iran's national Shetab (interbank switch) and Shaparak (card-acquiring) payment networks. Iran's Banking Coordination Council said no unauthorised access to customer accounts or banking databases was identified and that no data was deleted or altered; ISC announced on 24 June that all disruptions had been resolved. A group calling itself 'Black Wolves' claimed responsibility on Telegram, but former Israeli defence-intelligence advisor Ronen Solomon told The Telegraph the attacks bear the hallmarks of Predatory Sparrow (Gonjeshke Darande), widely assessed as a front for Israeli state cyber operations. The timing - days after the 17 June 2026 US-Iran ceasefire memorandum of understanding - led analysts to frame the strikes as possible covert pressure to disrupt the post-war agreement. The material GUARD event is an OPS business-continuity failure: a multi-day, nationwide loss of core retail-payment delivery across three of the country's systemically important banks plus the fuel-retail payment rail.

US forces strike Iranian missile, drone and radar sites after Strait of Hormuz drone attack breaches ceasefire

On 26 June 2026, six land-based US aircraft struck four Iranian targets — missile and drone storage locations and coastal radar/communications sites near the port of Sirik (Hormozgan province) and on Qeshm Island — in retaliation for a 25 June Iranian (IRGC) one-way attack-drone strike on the Singapore-flagged container vessel M/V Ever Lovely as it exited the Strait of Hormuz along the Omani coast. Iran reportedly launched at least four drones at shipping; one hit the Ever Lovely's upper deck (no crew injuries, vessel proceeded) while the US downed the other three. CENTCOM said the attack on commercial shipping "clearly violated the ceasefire" — the 17 June 2026 US-Iran memorandum of understanding that established a 60-day interim cessation and committed Iran to allow commercial vessel passage. President Trump called it a "foolish violation"; VP Vance warned further attacks would be met with force. Within 24 hours the IRGC Aerospace Force announced retaliatory strikes on the US airbase from which the operation originated, vowing a "broader" response to any repeat. The episode is the most serious test yet of the fragile ceasefire over the world's most important oil and LNG chokepoint (~20% of global seaborne oil and LNG), driving fresh volatility in Brent (up as much as ~4% intraday before easing), elevated war-risk insurance premiums, and continued shipping caution and Gulf airspace rerouting.

FBI and CISA warn Russian intelligence is hijacking Signal accounts by phishing victims out of their Backup Recovery Keys

On June 26, 2026 the FBI's IC3 and CISA issued an updated joint public service advisory (PSA I-062626-PSA) warning that Russian Intelligence Services (RIS) actors are running an enhanced social-engineering campaign against the Signal messaging app. Building on the original March 20, 2026 advisory (I-032026-PSA), the update discloses a new technique: operators impersonate automated Signal support and walk high-value targets through enabling Signal backups, opening their Backup Recovery Key, and pasting it into the chat — often framed as a mandatory two-factor rollout or an urgent 'data recovery' fix. With the key, an attacker can restore the victim's encrypted backup, read full private and group message history, and persistently take over the account. Critically, the stolen key remains valid even after the victim creates a new account on the same phone number, defeating naive remediation. The FBI attributes the activity to multiple RIS groups — FSB officers (including those embedded with FSB Border Guards) and Russian military personnel — and adds two public tracking names, UNC5792 and UNC4221 (CERT-UA: UAC-0195 / UAC-0185), both previously documented by Google/Mandiant as Russia-aligned actors abusing Signal's device-linking and group-invite features. Targets are individuals of strategic espionage value: current/former US and international government officials, military personnel, political figures, journalists, and Ukrainian officials. The only effective fix is generating a fresh Backup Recovery Key in Settings, which invalidates the stolen one for future backup downloads. This is intelligence-collection espionage rather than a product vulnerability — the cryptography is intact; the attack defeats the human, not the protocol.

Klue SaaS supply-chain breach widens; BeyondTrust and LastPass added to growing Salesforce-victim list as Icarus extortion group leaks CRM data

Vancouver-based competitive/market-intelligence SaaS vendor Klue was compromised via a dormant-but-active legacy "prototype" credential that had never been decommissioned. The attacker (the Icarus extortion group, active since ~April 2026) pushed a malicious code update to Klue's backend that harvested long-lived OAuth tokens connecting Klue to customers' Salesforce and Gong CRM environments. Using those tokens, Icarus queried the Salesforce REST API via automated Python scripts for roughly 24 hours — slow reconnaissance mapping objects, then ~1,000 queries in a 15-minute burst — exfiltrating CRM data (names, business emails, phone numbers, physical addresses, job titles, price quotes, sales notes, support case data and internal sales communications) across hundreds of downstream customers. Klue detected the activity on 2026-06-12 and alerted customers on 2026-06-13; Icarus publicly listed Klue on 2026-06-19. The incident kept widening through the reporting window: on 2026-06-26, BeyondTrust and LastPass were newly confirmed as impacted, joining HackerOne, Snyk, Tanium, Recorded Future, Huntress, Jamf, OneTrust, Sprout Social, Insurity, Gong, 8x8 and Pendo (~15 named victims). Salesforce and Gong disabled the Klue integration. Impacted parties (notably LastPass) stressed core products, vaults and infrastructure were NOT affected — only Salesforce-resident CRM/sales data. The attack mirrors the 2025 Salesloft Drift and Gainsight OAuth-abuse campaigns, confirming a now-repeatable SaaS-integration "OAuth playbook."

CISA adds actively exploited PTC Windchill RCE (CVE-2026-12569) to KEV as JSP web shell attacks continue

CVE-2026-12569, a CVSS 9.3 unauthenticated remote code execution flaw caused by unsafe deserialization of untrusted data in the web-based Windchill PDMLink component of PTC's Windchill and FlexPLM product lifecycle management (PLM) suites, is being actively exploited in the wild. PTC began releasing patches and mitigations on June 17, 2026 and published indicators of compromise on June 18; on June 25 it confirmed "continued reports of heightened threat activity." CISA added the flaw to its Known Exploited Vulnerabilities (KEV) catalog on June 25 (announced June 26) with a federal patch deadline of June 28, 2026 — the first-ever PTC product to enter the KEV catalog. Attackers are deploying persistent JSP web shells (pattern /Windchill/login/[0-9a-f]{16}.jsp) against internet-exposed instances to enable remote command execution and data exfiltration, with a known C2 at 5.180.41.35. Windchill/FlexPLM is the system of record for engineering BOMs, CAD data and product IP across automotive, aerospace, defense, electronics, medical-device, industrial-machinery and apparel/footwear supply chains; named users include BMW, Lockheed Martin, Boeing and NVIDIA. The exposure is essentially an IP-theft and supply-chain-compromise vector: a foothold in a PLM tenant exposes design files, supplier data and product roadmaps. This follows the March 2026 critical PTC flaw CVE-2026-4681 (CVSS 10.0) that prompted German police to physically warn companies, indicating an accelerating attacker focus on PLM/PDM platforms as a high-value, under-patched attack surface.

On 26 June 2026, six land-based US aircraft struck four Iranian targets — missile and drone storage locations and coastal radar/communications sites near the port of Sirik (Hormozgan province) and on Qeshm Island — in retaliation for a 25 June Iranian (IRGC) one-way attack-drone strike on the Singapore-flagged container vessel M/V Ever Lovely as it exited the Strait of Hormuz along the Omani coast. President Trump called it a "foolish violation"; VP Vance warned further attacks would be met with force. Within 24 hours the IRGC Aerospace Force announced retaliatory strikes on the US airbase from which the operation originated, vowing a "broader" response to any repeat. The episode is the most serious test yet of the fragile ceasefire over the world's most important oil and LNG chokepoint (~20% of global seaborne oil and LNG), driving fresh volatility in Brent (up as much as ~4% intraday before easing), elevated war-risk insurance premiums, and continued shipping caution and Gulf airspace rerouting.

FBI and CISA warn Russian intelligence is hijacking Signal accounts by phishing victims out of their Backup Recovery Keys

On June 26, 2026 the FBI's IC3 and CISA issued an updated joint public service advisory (PSA I-062626-PSA) warning that Russian Intelligence Services (RIS) actors are running an enhanced social-engineering campaign against the Signal messaging app. Building on the original March 20, 2026 advisory (I-032026-PSA), the update discloses a new technique: operators impersonate automated Signal support and walk high-value targets through enabling Signal backups, opening their Backup Recovery Key, and pasting it into the chat — often framed as a mandatory two-factor rollout or an urgent 'data recovery' fix. With the key, an attacker can restore the victim's encrypted backup, read full private and group message history, and persistently take over the account. Critically, the stolen key remains valid even after the victim creates a new account on the same phone number, defeating naive remediation. The FBI attributes the activity to multiple RIS groups — FSB officers (including those embedded with FSB Border Guards) and Russian military personnel — and adds two public tracking names, UNC5792 and UNC4221 (CERT-UA: UAC-0195 / UAC-0185), both previously documented by Google/Mandiant as Russia-aligned actors abusing Signal's device-linking and group-invite features. Targets are individuals of strategic espionage value: current/former US and international government officials, military personnel, political figures, journalists, and Ukrainian officials. The only effective fix is generating a fresh Backup Recovery Key in Settings, which invalidates the stolen one for future backup downloads. This is intelligence-collection espionage rather than a product vulnerability — the cryptography is intact; the attack defeats the human, not the protocol.

Klue SaaS supply-chain breach widens; BeyondTrust and LastPass added to growing Salesforce-victim list as Icarus extortion group leaks CRM data

Vancouver-based competitive/market-intelligence SaaS vendor Klue was compromised via a dormant-but-active legacy "prototype" credential that had never been decommissioned. The attacker (the Icarus extortion group, active since ~April 2026) pushed a malicious code update to Klue's backend that harvested long-lived OAuth tokens connecting Klue to customers' Salesforce and Gong CRM environments. Using those tokens, Icarus queried the Salesforce REST API via automated Python scripts for roughly 24 hours — slow reconnaissance mapping objects, then ~1,000 queries in a 15-minute burst — exfiltrating CRM data (names, business emails, phone numbers, physical addresses, job titles, price quotes, sales notes, support case data and internal sales communications) across hundreds of downstream customers. Klue detected the activity on 2026-06-12 and alerted customers on 2026-06-13; Icarus publicly listed Klue on 2026-06-19. The incident kept widening through the reporting window: on 2026-06-26, BeyondTrust and LastPass were newly confirmed as impacted, joining HackerOne, Snyk, Tanium, Recorded Future, Huntress, Jamf, OneTrust, Sprout Social, Insurity, Gong, 8x8 and Pendo (~15 named victims). Salesforce and Gong disabled the Klue integration. Impacted parties (notably LastPass) stressed core products, vaults and infrastructure were NOT affected — only Salesforce-resident CRM/sales data. The attack mirrors the 2025 Salesloft Drift and Gainsight OAuth-abuse campaigns, confirming a now-repeatable SaaS-integration "OAuth playbook."

'Cordyceps' CI/CD flaws expose 300+ exploitable GitHub repositories at Microsoft, Google, Apache, Cloudflare and Python Software Foundation to supply-chain takeover

On 24-25 June 2026, Novee Security (research led by founding engineer Elad Meged) publicly disclosed "Cordyceps," a systemic class of GitHub Actions CI/CD vulnerabilities that exists in the composition of workflows rather than in any single file. The pattern: a low-privilege workflow triggered by an untrusted pull request or PR comment passes attacker-controlled output into a high-privilege workflow whose token then authenticates to a cloud provider with maintainer/owner-level access. Each step looks benign in isolation; the danger only materializes when the full chain is traced across trust boundaries no one audited. Exploitation requires only a free GitHub account with no organization membership or special privileges. Of roughly 30,000 high-impact repositories scanned, 654 were flagged in a single automated pass and over 300 were confirmed fully exploitable for attacker-controlled code execution, credential theft, or outright supply-chain compromise. Demonstrated impacts: in Microsoft Azure Sentinel an anonymous PR comment executed attacker code on Microsoft CI infrastructure and stole a non-expiring GitHub App key granting persistent write access to security detection content shipped to customer workspaces; in Google's AI Agent Development Kit (adk-samples) a single malicious PR granted roles/owner over the associated Google Cloud project; in Apache Doris zero-click PR-comment attacks exfiltrated CI credentials/write tokens; in Cloudflare's Workers SDK malformed branch names triggered arbitrary command execution; and at Python's Black formatter (130M installs/month) any PR could steal the project bot token, enabling approval forgery and a downstream path to poisoning official Docker images. The research highlights that AI/agentic coding tools are mass-reproducing these insecure CI/CD patterns across millions of repositories. Microsoft and Google confirmed impact; Cloudflare, Python and Apache applied hardening and patches. No CVE identifiers were assigned (systemic misconfiguration class, not a single product CVE) and no financial impact was disclosed.

Symantec discloses 'Mistic' fileless self-destructing backdoor feeding six major ransomware operations via access broker KongTuke

On June 24-25, 2026, Broadcom's Symantec and the Carbon Black Threat Hunter Team disclosed a newly identified, memory-only backdoor named Mistic (tracked by Zscaler as MTLBackdoor / MLTBackdoor) used in financially motivated intrusions since April 2026 against insurance, education, IT-services and professional-services organizations. Mistic executes payloads entirely in process memory with no file written to disk, can load Beacon Object Files (BOFs) to extend its capabilities in-memory, and carries a built-in kill switch that terminates and deletes itself on command to erase forensic evidence. It is loaded via DLL side-loading: the legitimate signed binary MpExtMs.exe loads a malicious version.dll loader (which hooks GetModuleFileNameW and LoadLibraryW), which in turn loads EndpointDlp.dll — naming chosen to mimic Microsoft endpoint-security tooling. Symantec attributes Mistic to KongTuke (a.k.a. Woodgnat, and reported elsewhere under the overlapping clusters 404 TDS, Chaya_002, LandUpdate808 and TAG-124), an initial access broker active since at least May 2024 that establishes durable corporate footholds and sells them to ransomware affiliates. Mistic was deployed in close proximity to ModeloRAT, a Python RAT attributed to the same actor and delivered through ClickFix (and its FileFix/CrashFix variants) and Microsoft Teams social-engineering lures impersonating IT support, with malicious traffic distribution largely staged on compromised WordPress sites. Symantec has observed ModeloRAT used in attacks delivering Qilin ransomware, and the broker is linked to Qilin, Interlock, Rhysida, Akira, 8Base and Black Basta. Because the backdoor is fileless and self-erasing, traditional on-disk file scans miss it, raising systemic detection-gap risk across all sectors fed by this access-broker-to-ransomware pipeline. No CVEs are implicated; initial access relies on social engineering and credential/plugin compromise rather than a software vulnerability. No victim names, victim counts or financial losses were disclosed.

On June 23, 2026, a worldwide AI/semiconductor sell-off, amplified by the Federal Reserve's hawkish shift, drove US equities to their worst session of the year: the Nasdaq Composite fell ~2.2%, the S&P 500 ~1.4%, and the Philadelphia Semiconductor Index (SOX) ~8%. Memory names Micron and SanDisk fell ~12-13%; AMD, Intel and Marvell dropped 5.8-9.4%; Nvidia fell ~3-4%. The trigger was a repricing of Fed policy: the June 17 FOMC saw nine of 18 participants pencil in at least one 2026 rate hike, and markets moved to price two hikes by December (Bank of America projected three hikes to a 4.25-4.5% policy rate), compressing the discount-rate math under high-multiple, often unprofitable AI/growth equities (the so-called "Parabolic 7" momentum unwind / gamma squeeze). The standout casualty was SpaceX (SPCX). Fresh off the largest IPO in history (June 12, 2026: $135/share, ~$75B raised, ~$1.75-1.8T valuation), SPCX surged ~67% to an all-time high of $225.64 on June 16 — briefly the 4th-most-valuable US public company, passing Amazon and Microsoft — before collapsing. On June 23 it hit a post-IPO low of $147.11 and closed near $154.60, down ~16.4% on the day, wiping roughly $400 billion in market value in that single session (Fortune). The three-session slide (down ~5% Wed, ~3.6% Thu, ~16.4% Mon) cut the stock ~23-25% and erased ~$600 billion from the closing peak (~$920 billion measured from the intraday high). SpaceX's drop was not purely macro: it was compounded by idiosyncratic factors — a surprise large bond issuance (Bloomberg: at least $20B, to repay a bridge loan from the February 2026 xAI acquisition; the bond drew ~$89B demand) read by equity holders as a credit/liquidity signal, plus looming lock-up expirations (up to 44% insider unlock by early September, raising free float by roughly 900%). Notably, credit investors showed strong faith (heavy bond oversubscription) even as equity holders fled, a classic divergence. The selloff continued into reporting on June 24, with SPCX trading around $154.5. This is a material market-shock / market-risk event centered on AI valuation fragility and Fed policy, not a fraud, breach, or operational failure.

South Korea's KOSPI crashes 9.99% to 8,203.84, triggering a 20-minute circuit breaker as ~5 trillion won ($2.5B+) in foreign capital flees in AI-chip rout

On June 23, 2026 ("Black Tuesday"), South Korea's benchmark KOSPI index collapsed 9.99% to close at 8,203.84 — one of the five largest single-day percentage falls in the index's history — tripping a market-wide 20-minute circuit breaker on the Korea Exchange after the index breached the -8% threshold around 2:33pm KST. Memory-chip giants Samsung Electronics (-12.31%) and SK Hynix (-12.47%), which together represent roughly half of the index's market capitalization, drove the collapse; the KOSDAQ fell ~7.9% in parallel. Foreign investors offloaded roughly 5 trillion won of Korean equities in the single session (widely reported as $2.5B+ in net foreign outflow), with institutions also heavy net sellers. Three catalysts converged: MSCI's June 23 decision to again keep South Korea off its Developed Markets upgrade watchlist (citing won offshore-convertibility and operational/market-access barriers), Korean regulators (FSS/FSC) flagging risk in newly approved leveraged single-stock ETFs tied to Samsung and SK Hynix that had ballooned to ~14 trillion won in assets with ~92% retail ownership, and forced liquidations among leveraged retail investors amid a broad global AI-stock repricing following the Fed's hawkish June 17 meeting. The shock rippled worldwide — Japan's Nikkei 225 fell 3.55%, Kioxia tumbled >15%, the Nasdaq Composite fell 2.21%, Micron fell ~13%, and the Philadelphia Semiconductor Index shed ~8%. The KOSPI rebounded ~3.27% on June 24 on a Yonhap-reported ~90 trillion won ($58.6B) Samsung buyback rumor and SK Hynix's plan to raise ~$29B via Nasdaq depositary receipts.

Morgan keeps them; Hertz gets only a nominal lending fee). KOSPI crashes 9.99% on 'Black Tuesday,' triggers market-wide circuit breaker as AI-memory bubble fears erupt

On Tuesday June 23, 2026 ('Black Tuesday'), South Korea's benchmark KOSPI index plunged 9.99% to close at 8,203.84, down 910.71 points — its largest single-day point loss ever and the fifth-largest percentage drop in index history. The Korea Exchange triggered intraday trading curbs in sequence: a KOSDAQ program-trade sidecar at ~11:37 a.m., a KOSPI sell-sidecar at ~11:40 a.m., and finally a Level 1 market-wide circuit breaker at 2:33 p.m. that halted all KOSPI trading for 20 minutes after the index stayed more than 8% below the prior close for one minute. The KOSDAQ fell ~8%, breaking below 900. The rout was driven by a cluster of simultaneous catalysts: (1) AI-memory/HBM expansion-slowdown fears crystallised by Broadcom's June 4 guidance miss (its ~$16B Q3 AI-chip forecast missed the ~$17.2B estimate; Broadcom fell ~12.6%, erasing ~$286B in value), after Korean memory valuations had risen 800%+ in 12 months; (2) a hawkish June 17 FOMC dot plot (9 of 19 officials projecting at least one 2026 hike, median end-year funds rate 3.8%) lifting discount rates; (3) fading hopes for MSCI Developed-Markets inclusion after the index review excluded South Korea; (4) extreme retail leverage with margin debt near a record 37.7–38 trillion won feeding forced liquidations; and (5) domestic policy fears over taxing unrealized capital gains and large-scale National Pension Service rebalancing. Samsung Electronics (~-12.3%) and SK Hynix (~-12.5%) — together ~48% of KOSPI market value and ~70% of the index's 2026 gains — concentrated the damage. Foreign investors net-sold ~5.79 trillion won (~$3.8B) as the won weakened toward ~1,560/USD, a level rarely seen in 15+ years. Contagion spread regionally (Nikkei 225 down ~3.6%, SoftBank -15%, CSI 300 -2.8%) and to the US (Nasdaq -2.21%, S&P 500 -1.44%). Despite the crash, Korean retail investors bought a record net ~11.11 trillion won, signalling continued conviction in the AI narrative. This is an accidental/structural market dislocation (no malicious actor), rooted in extreme index concentration, leverage, and AI-valuation reflexivity.

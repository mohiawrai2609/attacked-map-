# Attacked.ai — Complete Product Explainer
### Plain-English, end-to-end guide for a client meeting
*Covers: what the product is · the Landing Page · the Attack Map · the Attacked Hub · every concept (blast radius, adaptive controls, peer watchlist, vendor ratings, GUARD) · how the data works · who it's for.*

---

## 1. The one-sentence version

**Attacked.ai is a daily, global "risk radar" for businesses.** Every day it collects real-world incidents — cyber attacks, data breaches, financial shocks, geopolitical events, supply-chain failures, physical disasters — and turns each one into a clear, structured briefing that answers three questions a leader actually cares about:

1. **What happened?**
2. **Who else does this put at risk — including possibly *me*?** (the "blast radius")
3. **What should I do about it?** (the "adaptive controls")

Think of it as *Bloomberg Terminal meets a security analyst*, but readable by a normal executive, not just a technical team.

---

## 2. Why it exists (the problem)

Most companies find out about a risk **too late** — after it has already hit them or a partner. The news tells you "Company X was breached," but it never tells you:

- *Are my suppliers, customers, or competitors exposed to the same thing?*
- *Does this pattern apply to my industry or my region?*
- *What concrete step should I take this week to not be next?*

Attacked.ai exists to answer exactly those questions, **every day**, in plain language, with the connections drawn for you.

## 3. The three surfaces (how the whole product fits together)

The product is one platform with **three "front doors,"** each for a different moment:

| Surface | What it is | Who it's for | The job it does |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Landing Page** | The public marketing front page | A first-time visitor / prospect | "What is this and why should I sign up?" |
| **Attacked Hub** | A daily news-style feed of all incidents | Anyone browsing the intelligence | "Show me everything that happened, like a newspaper" |
| **Attack Map** | An interactive globe of live incidents | Signed-in operators / analysts | "Let me explore the deep detail — who's exposed and what to do" |

The natural journey: a visitor lands on the **Landing Page** → browses headlines in the **Hub** → signs up → works the deep detail in the **Map**.

They share one brand, one data source, and one classification system (**GUARD**), so they feel like one product.

## 4. GUARD — the classification system behind everything

**GUARD is the framework that gives every incident a consistent label.** Without it, you'd have a messy pile of news. With it, every incident is sorted the same way, every day, so you can filter, compare, and trend it.

Two things GUARD assigns to every incident:

### a) A risk **category** (the "what kind of risk")
There are **13 categories**:

| Code | Category | Plain meaning |
|---|---|---|
| CYB | Cyber | Hacks, ransomware, intrusions |
| DAT | Data & Privacy | Data leaks, privacy breaches |
| FIN | Financial | Fraud, market shocks, financial loss |
| GEO | Geopolitical | War, sanctions, state conflict |
| OPS | Operations | Operational failures, outages |
| PHY | Physical Security | Physical attacks, facility/infrastructure damage |
| PPL | People | Workforce, insider, safety-of-people events |
| REG | Regulatory | Fines, investigations, new rules |
| REP | Reputation | Brand/PR damage |
| STR | Strategic | Strategic/competitive threats |
| TEC | Technology | Tech failures, platform risk |
| TPR | Third Party | Supplier / vendor / partner risk |
| ENV | Environmental | Climate, natural disasters, environmental harm |

*(An incident can touch several categories — e.g. a hospital ransomware case is Cyber + Operations + Third-Party + People.)*

### b) A **severity** score (the "how bad")
A simple **5-tier scale**, colour-coded everywhere in the product:

| Level | Label | Colour | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| 5 | **Critical** | Red | Immediate, severe |
| 4 | **High** | Orange | Urgent |
| 3 | **Medium** | Gold | Monitor closely |
| 2 | **Low** | Green | Routine |
| 1 | **Minimal** | Grey | Informational |

So when you see a small coloured chip like **`CRITICAL · CYBER`**, that's GUARD telling you, at a glance, *how bad* and *what kind*.

## 5. The Landing Page — "what is this?"

This is the public front page a stranger sees first. Its only job is to **explain the value in 10 seconds and get a sign-up.**

What's on it, top to bottom:

- **Headline:** *"Every incident. Every blast radius. Mapped."* — the promise in one line.
- **A live rotating globe** — a visual signal that this is global, live, and map-based.
- **A real live incident card** — pulled straight from today's database, so the page is never stale; it proves the product is alive and current.
- **A short explanation** of what the product does (cyber, financial, geopolitical, physical… classified through GUARD, geolocated, written for operators).
- **Two calls to action:** *"View the live map"* and *"Get the daily brief."*
- **A capabilities section** (numbered) explaining the core ideas, **newsletter sign-up**, and a **pricing strip** (free to enter, deeper tiers behind sign-up).

Everything on it (the numbers, the sample incident) is **fetched live** — nothing is hard-coded — so it always reflects the real, current state of the platform.

## 6. The Attacked Hub — "the daily newspaper of risk"

The Hub is the **editorial feed**: every incident, laid out like a premium news site (think *Financial Times*), but in our brand.

### What it looks like
- A **category bar** across the top (All · Cyber · Financial · Geopolitical …). Click one to filter the feed to just that risk type.
- A **lead story** (the biggest incident), an **"Editor's Picks"** rail, **"Top Stories,"** per-category **section bands**, **"GUARD Analysis"** opinion pieces, a **"Most Critical Today"** list, and a **"Latest Intelligence"** grid you can page through.
- Each item is a **card**: a photo, a coloured severity chip, the category, the headline, the company/country, and a one-line summary.

### What a "card" is
A **card is the short preview of one incident** — the headline-level summary you scan. Clicking a card opens the **full briefing** (the deep report — see §8). So: *cards = the scannable list; the report = the full story.*

### Why it matters in the meeting
The Hub is the surface that feels familiar and shareable — a client immediately "gets" it because it reads like a news site, but every story is structured, classified, and connected to action.

## 7. The Attack Map — "the situation room"

The Map is the **interactive, exploratory surface** — a 3D globe (you can switch to a flat map) with every incident plotted at its real location. This is where the *deep* intelligence lives. Each glowing dot is an incident, colour-coded by severity.

You **drag to spin, scroll to zoom, and click a dot** to open its full intelligence. The moment you click an incident, several panels appear around the screen — each one answering a different operator question. Those panels are the heart of the product, so here they are in plain English:

### 7.1 The incident pin / marker
A coloured dot on the globe. Colour = severity (red = critical, etc.). It sits at the incident's real-world location. Clicking it "opens" the incident.

### 7.2 GUARD Classification panel — *"what is this, officially?"*
The structured label: the category, severity, sub-type, date, the entity involved, and **why** it got that severity. This is the "file header" for the incident.

### 7.3 Blast Radius — *"who else should care?"* ⭐ (the signature feature)
**This is the idea that makes Attacked.ai different.** A normal news story stops at "Company X got hit." **Blast radius** keeps going and maps **everyone else the incident touches** — the ripple effect.

For each incident it lists the connected parties and *why they're exposed*, grouped into rings such as:
- **Internal** — the directly hit organisation
- **Supply chain** — its suppliers/vendors
- **Peers** — similar companies likely exposed to the same thing
- **Regulators** — bodies who will investigate or impose rules
- **Customers** — downstream businesses that depend on it
- **Capital** — investors, insurers, financial stakeholders

The panel headline is literally *"Who else should care."* It answers the executive's real fear: **"Could this reach me?"** Each entity has a short reason (e.g. *"primary affected provider," "supplier of the compromised component," "regulator running the investigation"*) and its own severity.

> **Meeting soundbite:** *"Everyone reports the explosion. We map the blast radius — every company, supplier, regulator and investor caught in the ripple."*

### 7.4 Adaptive Controls — *"what do I actually do?"* ⭐
Knowing you're at risk is useless without an action. **Adaptive controls are the recommended, concrete steps** to reduce your exposure to *this specific* incident. "Adaptive" because they're tailored to the incident's pattern — not generic checklist advice.

Example: for a software-supply-chain attack, a control might be *"disable automatic task execution for untrusted code repositories"* — a specific, do-it-this-week action. They map to recognised security frameworks so a security team can act on them immediately.

> **Meeting soundbite:** *"For every threat we don't just raise the alarm — we hand you the specific control that closes the door."*

### 7.5 Peer Watchlist — *"companies showing similar risk"*
A list of **other companies exhibiting the same warning signs** as the incident — your "watch these next" list. It's the early-warning angle: if these peers show the same signals, they (or you, if you're like them) may be next. (First few are shown to everyone; the full list is a paid/design-partner feature.)

### 7.6 Vendor Defence Ratings
Where relevant, the incident is connected to **security vendors/tools** and how well-positioned they are against this threat — useful for "which solution actually helps here?" conversations.

### 7.7 Sweep Archive — *"every day, stored"*
The Map keeps a **day-by-day archive** of intelligence "sweeps." Each day's file of incidents is stored and listed (e.g. *"Mon 29 Jun 2026 · 22 incidents"*), and you can click any past day to replay that day's incidents on the globe. This gives you **history and trend** — not just today.

### 7.8 Controls along the bottom and sides (the "layers")
- **Flat / Globe** — switch between a 3D globe and a flat world map.
- **Buyer / Newsroom** — two viewing modes (a buyer/operator lens vs a news lens).
- **Labels / Heat / Blast** — overlays: show place labels, a heat map of concentration, or the blast-radius connection lines.
- **Filters** — narrow the map by severity, category, or industry.

> **Meeting soundbite:** *"The Hub is the newspaper. The Map is the situation room — you spin the globe, click an incident, and instantly see who's exposed and what to do."*

## 8. The incident report (the full briefing)

When you open an incident anywhere (a Hub card or a Map pin), you get the **full briefing** — a single, rich, designed report. It reads like a top-tier analyst note and includes (depending on the incident):

- **Standfirst & narrative** — the story, written for operators.
- **Executive bullets / takeaway** — the "if you only read one thing" summary.
- **Severity & rationale** — how bad, and *why*.
- **Blast radius** — the connected parties (as in §7.3).
- **Attack / event chain** — how it unfolded, step by step.
- **Causes** — what actually went wrong.
- **Scenarios & outlook** — what could happen next.
- **Comparables** — similar past incidents to learn from.
- **GUARD control mapping & frameworks** — the controls and the standards they map to.
- **Dossier, metrics, vendors, sources** — supporting detail, the numbers, and **citations** (every claim is sourced).
- **Byline** — authored/attributed, like real journalism.

These full reports are written and "baked" for each incident, so each briefing is a polished, shareable, **PDF-downloadable** document — not just a database row.

## 9. How the data stays fresh (in plain English)

- Every day, a **"sweep"** gathers the day's incidents from real sources and runs them through the **GUARD** classification (category + severity + blast radius + controls).
- The results are stored in a secure cloud database (**Supabase**).
- All three surfaces (Landing, Hub, Map) read from that live data — so the moment a sweep lands, the whole product updates. **Nothing is hard-coded or stale.**
- The Map's **archive** keeps each day, so you build up history and can look back.

*Scale today: hundreds of fully-written incident briefings across 13 categories and dozens of countries and industries, growing daily.*

## 11. Glossary (hand this to anyone)

- **GUARD** — our framework that classifies every incident by **category** (13 types) and **severity** (1–5).
- **Severity** — how serious: Critical / High / Medium / Low / Minimal (red → grey).
- **Incident** — a single real-world event we've captured and briefed.
- **Card** — the short preview of an incident in a list/feed.
- **Briefing / Report** — the full, written, sourced document for an incident.
- **Blast radius** — *everyone else* an incident puts at risk (suppliers, peers, regulators, customers, investors), with the reason for each.
- **Adaptive controls** — the specific, tailored actions to reduce your exposure to that incident.
- **Peer watchlist** — other companies showing the same warning signs ("who's next").
- **Vendor defence ratings** — how well security vendors/tools counter the threat.
- **Sweep** — a day's batch of collected, classified incidents.
- **Sweep archive** — the day-by-day history of sweeps you can replay on the map.
- **Layers (Flat/Globe, Buyer/Newsroom, Labels/Heat/Blast)** — ways to view the map.
